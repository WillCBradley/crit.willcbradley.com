| Enzymes

Chemical reactions require energy.

Even exothermic reactions (like explosions) need an initial nudge before they can begin.

We call this nudge the "activation energy" of a reaction; the higher a reaction's activation energy, the more work required to get things started.

This is where enyzmes come in.

Their specialised protein structures bind to certain reaction ingredients ("reactants"), decreasing activation energy.

In turn, lower activation energy facilitates chemical reactions that keep us alive.

Plus, they play an important role when it comes to medications:


| Pharmacokinetics (PK)

PK is often described as "what the body does to a drug".

Its four main pillars are absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion.

Let's quickly go through each.


Absorption

Absorption is the active or passive movement of a drug into the bloodstream after it's been administered.

It depends on properties of the drug (e.g. size, charge, lipid solubility), and of the body (e.g. GI surface area for oral drugs, or tissue perfusion for IM injections).

But even once a drug reaches the blood, it might not stay there:


Distribution

Distribution describes the movement of a drug between the blood and other tissues.

This can be quantified as volume of distribution (VD); higher VD means more ready distribution into tissues, and hence a higher dose required for any given plasma concentration.

Distribution is variable based on many factors, including body composition (e.g. relative volumes of fat vs fluid), and drug properties (e.g. relative solubility of the drug in fat vs fluid).

Regardless, most drugs meet the same fate.


Metabolism

Metabolism is when the body chemically alters drugs, often into forms that can be excreted.

It primarily occurs in the liver across two main phases:

  • Phase I (Functionalisation) — The addition of small groups of atoms ("functional groups") to the chemical structure of a drug in preparation for Phase II.
  • Phase II (Conjugation) — The linking of functionalised drug molecules to a other endogenous molecules, typically making it more water soluble.

The resultant molecules ("metabolites") can be active (meaning they exert some kind of pharmacological effect), inactive (mean they don't), or even toxic (where they can cause direct harm).

But in any case, we eventually move on to the final step:


Excretion

Excretion is the process by which the body gets rid of drugs.

It tends to occur in the liver (through bile) and the kidneys (through urine), but drugs can also be excreted through breath, sweat, saliva or breast milk.

Two drug properties are relevant here:

  • Clearance (Cl) — Volume of blood plasma cleared of drug per unit time
  • Half Life (t1⁄2) — Time taken for active drug concentration to reduce by 50%

But we're getting ahead of ourselves.

Before excretion, drugs need to exert their effects. Let's explore this in more detail.



| Pharmacodynamics (PD)

In contrast to PK, PD describes "what a drug does to the body".

Obviously, this a broad topic, but there are key principles we can refer back to:


Receptor Theory

Cells are complex.

At any given moment, they can be undergoing huge, cascading chains of chemical reactions.

Which begs the question: how do drugs exert their desired effect amidst all this chaos?

This is where receptor theory comes in.

It states that drugs bind to specific, structurally-compatible cell components ("receptors"), forming a receptor-drug complex that initiates downstream biological effects.

The attraction ("affinity") of different drugs to different receptors, as can the "specificity" with which they bind to any given site (as opposed to all other sites).

And once bound, they can exert various effects:

  • Agonists — These drugs exert downstream effects
  • Antagonists — These drugs exert no effect, and prevent agonists from binding
  • Modulators — These change receptor-agonist affinity

But these changes aren't binary. Even once a drug binds to a receptor, many other factors determine the exact effect produced.


Concentration-Effect Relationships

This does what it says on the tin: describes the relationship between drug concentration and exerted effect.

The greater the effect per unit concentration the higher a drugs "potency". And the higher the maximum effect, the greater its "efficacy".

Based on concentration-effect relationships, we can produce a "dose-response curve".

And within this curve, a "therapeutic window" can be defined; this is the set of concentrations thought to balance the risk of adverse effects with the potential for therapeutic benefit.

Which brings us to dosing.


| Dosing

Let's consider two kinds of dosing: loading and maintenance.

Loading doses are designed to reach a certain concentration, and maintenance doses are designed to keep us there.

We can calculate these doses as follows:

Loading Dose = (Cdesired x VD) / Bioavailability

Maintenance Dose = Cdesired x Cl x Time
